Residency Permit

İkamet İzni

"What are the requirements and types of residency permits available for foreigners seeking to live or conduct business in Turkey?"

Quick Answer

A Residency Permit (İkamet İzni) is an official authorization allowing a foreigner to legally stay in Turkey beyond the standard visa or visa-exemption period (usually 90 days). Regulated by the Law on Foreigners and International Protection No. 6458 (YUKK), it comes in several forms depending on the purpose of stay, such as short-term, long-term, family, and student permits. Notably, a valid Turkish Work Permit automatically functions as a residence permit.

Legal Framework: Law on Foreigners (YUKK)

The core legal framework governing the freedom of residence and travel for foreigners in Turkey is the Law on Foreigners and International Protection No. 6458 (YUKK), which entered into force in 2013, effectively repealing the outdated 1950 Residence and Travel Law. This modern legislation brought Turkish immigration law closer to international standards and consolidated previously scattered regulations.

Under Article 16 of the Turkish Constitution, the fundamental rights and freedoms of foreigners—including the freedom of residence and travel—can be restricted by law in accordance with international law. YUKK outlines the specific conditions under which a foreigner may enter, reside in, and be expelled from Turkey.

The Concept: Domicile vs. Residence

In Turkish law, there is an important distinction between Domicile (İkametgâh) and Residence (İkamet). Under the Turkish Civil Code, a domicile is a place where a person lives with the intention of remaining permanently. However, for the purposes of immigration law under YUKK, "residence" simply refers to the legal status of staying in Turkey. Obtaining a residence permit does not automatically prove a permanent "intention to settle" under civil law, though certain permits (like the Long-Term Residence Permit) strongly imply such an intent.

Exemptions from Residence Permits

Not all foreigners need a residence permit. Under YUKK Article 20, the following individuals are exempt:

  • Foreigners staying within the duration of their visa or visa exemption (typically up to 90 days within a 180-day period).
  • Stateless persons holding a "Stateless Person Identity Document".
  • Holders of valid Work Permits (as the work permit counts as a residence permit).
  • Diplomatic and consular officials assigned to Turkey.
  • Holders of the Mavi Kart (Blue Card).

Types of Residence Permits

YUKK systematically regulates six distinct types of residence permits, tailored to the foreigner's specific circumstances:

  • Short-Term Residence Permit (Kısa Dönem İkamet İzni): The most common permit. Granted for purposes such as scientific research, establishing commercial connections or businesses, purchasing immovable residential property, tourism, medical treatment, or participating in Turkish language courses. Generally issued for a maximum of 2 years at a time, though exceptions exist for investors (up to 5 years).
  • Family Residence Permit (Aile İkamet İzni): Issued to the foreign spouse, minor children, or dependent children of a "sponsor" (who can be a Turkish citizen, Blue Card holder, or a foreigner with a valid residence/work permit). It is granted for up to 3 years at a time but cannot exceed the duration of the sponsor's permit.
  • Student Residence Permit (Öğrenci İkamet İzni): For foreigners enrolled in higher education programs (Associate, Bachelor's, Master's, PhD) in Turkey. Students are permitted to work in Turkey, provided they obtain a separate work permit.
  • Long-Term Residence Permit (Uzun Dönem İkamet İzni): An indefinite permit granted to foreigners who have continuously resided in Turkey for at least 8 years with valid residence permits, provided they meet strict criteria (e.g., no social assistance received in the past 3 years, adequate income, valid health insurance, and no threat to public order). Holders enjoy almost identical rights to Turkish citizens, excluding political rights (voting) and mandatory military service.
  • Humanitarian Residence Permit (İnsani İkamet İzni): Granted in extraordinary circumstances where the foreigner cannot be deported due to non-refoulement principles, severe health issues, or to protect the best interests of a child. Maximum duration is 1 year per issuance.
  • Victim of Human Trafficking Residence Permit: An initial 30-day permit granted to victims of human trafficking to allow them time to recover and decide whether to cooperate with authorities. It can be extended for 6-month periods, up to a maximum of 3 years.

Application, Rejection, and Cancellation

Applications are primarily initiated online via the e-İkamet system and finalized by the Provincial Directorate of Migration Management. The administration evaluates whether the applicant fulfills the specific requirements of the requested permit type, possesses adequate financial means, holds valid health insurance, and has suitable accommodation.

A residence permit application can be rejected, or an existing permit canceled, if:

  • The foreigner fails to meet or maintain the required conditions.
  • The permit is found to have been used for purposes other than those for which it was issued.
  • The permit was obtained using fraudulent documents.
  • There is a valid entry ban or deportation decision against the foreigner.
  • The foreigner stays outside of Turkey for durations exceeding statutory limits (interruption of residence).

Decisions of rejection or cancellation can be challenged in Turkish Administrative Courts within specified legal timeframes.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I work in Turkey with a standard residence permit?

No. A residence permit (ikamet izni) solely grants you the right to stay in Turkey. To work legally, you must obtain a separate Work Permit (Çalışma İzni) from the Ministry of Labor and Social Security. Notably, a valid work permit automatically functions as a residence permit, so you do not need both.

Does owning property in Turkey guarantee a residence permit?

Foreigners who own immovable property in Turkey can apply for a short-term residence permit. However, the property must be a residential dwelling and used for that purpose. While property ownership forms a strong basis for the application, the Directorate General of Migration Management still evaluates security, public order, and other statutory requirements before approval.

What happens if my residence permit expires while I am in Turkey?

Staying in Turkey after your residence permit expires without applying for an extension constitutes an overstay. You will be subject to administrative fines when leaving the country, and depending on the length of the overstay, you may face an entry ban (deportation order) preventing you from returning to Turkey for a specified period.

What is the 8-year rule for a Long-Term Residence Permit?

To qualify for a long-term residence permit, a foreigner must have continuously legally resided in Turkey for at least 8 years with a valid residence permit. Time spent outside of Turkey exceeding the allowed statutory limits may interrupt this continuous period, resetting the 8-year clock.
