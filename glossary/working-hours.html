Working Hours

"What are the legal working hour limits in Turkey?"

Quick Answer

Maximum working week is 45 hours, typically spread over 6 days. Daily limit is 11 hours. Flexible arrangements like compressed weeks are permitted.

Overview of Working Hours under Turkish Labor Law

Under Turkish Labor Law No. 4857, "working time" is a fundamental legal concept defining the period during which an employee performs or is considered to perform their duties arising from the employment contract. The regulation of working hours is vital not only for determining wage obligations but also for protecting employee health, ensuring workplace safety, and maintaining a work-life balance.

The Definition and Scope of Working Time

Turkish legislation does not just consider the time an employee is actively performing a physical or mental task as working time. The legal framework divides working time into two main categories: Factual Working Time and Statutory (Assumed) Working Time.

1. Factual Working Time (Fiili Çalışma Süresi)

This is the standard understanding of working hours. It covers the actual period from the beginning to the end of the shift where the employee is actively engaged in their duties under the employer's management and instruction.

2. Statutory or Assumed Working Time (Farazi Çalışma Süresi)

Article 66 of Labor Law No. 4857 explicitly defines certain periods as working time, even if the employee is not actively working. These are designed to protect the employee when their time is restricted by the employer's authority. Key examples include:

  • Travel Time for External Assignments: Time spent traveling to a location outside the primary workplace for an assignment given by the employer.
  • Waiting and On-Call Time: Time spent by the employee waiting for work or remaining at the employer's disposal at the workplace, even if no immediate task is assigned.
  • Nursing Leaves: Time granted to female employees for nursing their young children.
  • Descending/Ascending Time: In mining and underwater operations, the time spent descending to the actual work area or ascending from it.

Times Not Considered Working Hours

It is equally important to know what does not count towards the statutory working hours. The primary exclusion is Rest Breaks (Ara Dinlenmesi). Under Article 68, standard meal and rest breaks are not considered part of the working time. However, if an employee is required to remain at their workstation and cannot freely use their rest break, that time must be compensated as working time.

International Influence and EU Standards

The Turkish working hours framework is heavily influenced by International Labor Organization (ILO) conventions and European Union (EU) directives. For instance, the EU Working Time Directive (2003/88/EC) and landmark decisions by the European Court of Justice (like the SIMAP and Jaeger cases) have shaped how "on-call" time at the workplace is treated—reinforcing that time spent at the workplace under the employer's authority, even while resting during an on-call shift, must be classified as working time to protect employee health.

Compliance Risk Indicators

  • Misclassifying Wait Times: Failing to count mandatory waiting periods or on-site on-call shifts as paid working time is a frequent cause of employee wage claims.
  • Rest Break Violations: Requiring employees to perform light duties during their unpaid rest breaks converts that time into compensable working hours and may trigger overtime liabilities.
  • Travel Commutes: While standard home-to-work commuting is not working time, travel to alternative sites ordered by the employer must be tracked and compensated.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

What is the maximum legal working week in Turkey?

Under Labor Law No. 4857, the maximum regular working week is 45 hours. Unless agreed otherwise, this time is divided equally among the days worked in the week (e.g., 7.5 hours over 6 days). Any time worked beyond 45 hours in a week is legally considered overtime and must be compensated at a premium rate.

Are rest breaks included in the 45-hour workweek limit?

No. Rest breaks (ara dinlenmesi) are explicitly excluded from daily and weekly working hours. For example, an employee at the workplace from 08:00 to 17:00 (9 hours) with a 1-hour unpaid lunch break has a legal working time of 8 hours for that day.

Is commuting time considered working time in Turkey?

Ordinary commuting time from an employee's home to their regular workplace is not considered working time, even if the employer provides a shuttle service. However, if the employer sends the employee to work at a different location (e.g., another city or a client site), the travel time to that location is counted as working time.

How is "on-call" time treated under Turkish Law?

If an employee is required to remain at the workplace or a location designated by the employer to wait for work (e.g., an on-call doctor at a hospital), that entire duration is considered working time, regardless of whether they actually perform tasks. If they are on-call from home and free to manage their time until called, only the actual time spent working upon being called is considered working time.
