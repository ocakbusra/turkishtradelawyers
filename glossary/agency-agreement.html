Agency Agreement

"What are the legal requirements for agency agreements in Turkey?"

Quick Answer

Translated global templates routinely overlook the mandatory provisions of Turkish agency law. The Turkish Commercial Code (TCC) governs agency agreements, and there are critical clauses that drive most disputes, such as portfolio compensation, exclusivity limits, and termination notice periods.

Overview of Agency Agreements under Turkish Law

As businesses expand into new economic markets outside their headquarters, they often require cost-effective merchant assistants. Rather than opening new branches or hiring dependent sales representatives—which can be financially burdensome—many commercial enterprises meet this need through an Agency Agreement.

While the Turkish Commercial Code (TCC) No. 6102 does not explicitly define the "agency agreement" itself, it comprehensively defines the "agent" and regulates the rights and obligations of the parties involved. Under Article 102 of the TCC, an agent is defined as an independent person or entity who, based on a contract and acting as a profession, continuously mediates in contracts concerning a commercial enterprise within a specific place or region, or concludes them on behalf of that merchant, without having a dependent legal position such as a commercial representative, commercial proxy, or sales clerk.

Key Elements of an Agency Relationship

To qualify as an agency under Turkish law, the relationship must possess several core elements:

  • Independence: Unlike a commercial representative or employee, the agent is not bound by a dependent employment relationship to the principal's enterprise.
  • Contractual Basis: The relationship must be founded on an underlying contract between the principal and the agent.
  • Specific Territory or Region: The agent operates within a defined geographical area or customer segment.
  • Continuity: The mediation or contract-making activities must be continuous, distinguishing it from ad-hoc brokerage.
  • Commercial Enterprise: The contracts mediated must concern a commercial enterprise.
  • Professional Activity: The agent must undertake these activities as their profession.

Types of Agents in Turkey

Agents can be categorized based on their roles and the specific commercial sector they operate in:

  • Mediating Agents vs. Contracting Agents: Some agents merely mediate and prepare contracts for the principal to sign, while others have the explicit authority to conclude contracts directly on behalf of the principal.
  • Sector-Specific Agents: Beyond general commercial agents, Turkish law recognizes specific types such as sales and distribution agents, insurance agents, travel agents, and capital market agents, each subject to additional sector-specific regulations (e.g., the Insurance Law or the Travel Agencies Law).

Differences from Similar Commercial Contracts

It is crucial to distinguish an agency agreement from other forms of commercial representation:

  • Commercial Representatives and Proxies: These are dependent assistants acting under the direct control and employment of the principal, whereas agents are independent businesses.
  • Brokerage (Simsarlık): A broker acts on an ad-hoc or transaction-by-transaction basis without the element of continuity that characterizes an agency.
  • Commission Agents: A commission agent acts in their own name but on the principal's behalf. An agent acts in the principal's name and on the principal's behalf.
  • Exclusive Distributors and Franchisees: Distributors and franchisees buy and sell goods in their own name and on their own account, bearing the primary financial risk of the sales, unlike agents who mediate sales for the principal.

Rights and Obligations of the Agent

The TCC outlines a balanced framework of rights and obligations for both parties:

Agent's Rights:

  • Right to Remuneration: The agent has the right to a commission for transactions concluded through their mediation or within their exclusive territory.
  • Reimbursement of Extraordinary Expenses: While ordinary business expenses are borne by the agent, extraordinary expenses incurred for the principal's benefit must be reimbursed.
  • Right to Interest and Lien: Agents have a statutory right to claim interest on delayed payments and a right of lien (retention) over the principal's goods or funds under their control to secure unpaid commissions.
  • Monopoly/Exclusivity Right: Unless agreed otherwise in writing, an agent enjoys exclusivity in their designated territory or customer group.

Agent's Obligations:

  • Duty to Protect Principal's Interests: The agent must act diligently and protect the commercial interests of the principal.
  • Duty to Inform: The agent must keep the principal informed of market conditions and any transactions mediated.
  • Duty to Take Precautions: The agent must take necessary legal and practical steps to protect the principal's rights against third parties.

Termination and Its Consequences

An agency agreement may end due to mutual agreement, expiration of the term, termination by notice, or unforeseen circumstances such as bankruptcy, death, or loss of capacity. The termination triggers significant legal consequences:

  • Equalization Claim (Portfolio Compensation): Introduced more robustly in the new TCC (Article 122), this allows the agent to claim compensation for the customer base they built which the principal continues to benefit from after termination.
  • Post-Contractual Non-Compete (Restraint of Trade): The TCC permits reasonable non-compete clauses post-termination, provided the agent is adequately compensated for this restriction.

3 Critical Clauses Your Agency Agreement Might Be Missing

When adapting foreign agency agreements for the Turkish market, several mandatory provisions must be carefully addressed. Audit your contract against the three clauses that drive most disputes.

1. Portfolio Compensation (Clientele Fee) - Article 122 TCC

The Risk: Upon termination, the agent may claim portfolio compensation calculated up to the average of the last five years' annual commissions. This right cannot be waived in advance — any waiver clause is void under Turkish law.

Remediation: Your agreement must define how the customer portfolio is measured and apportioned. While the right is non-waivable, defining the measurement methodology narrows the calculation basis and limits exposure. Without it, you remain exposed to a discretionary court calculation up to the five-year average commission ceiling.

2. Defined Territory & Exclusivity Limits - Article 104 TCC

The Risk: Unless explicitly stated otherwise, an agent is presumed to have exclusivity within their designated territory or sector. Direct sales without a reservation clause may still trigger full commission obligations to the agent.

Remediation: Your agreement should explicitly reserve your right to conclude direct sales in the agent's territory. This express reservation rebuts the default exclusivity presumption under Article 104 TCC, significantly reducing double-payment risk.

3. Mandatory Termination Notice Periods - Article 121 TCC

The Risk: Short-notice termination clauses valid in other jurisdictions (e.g., 14-day notice) are typically void in Türkiye. The statutory three-month minimum applies, and shorter contractual periods expose the principal to wrongful termination liability.

Remediation: Ensure your contract aligns with the statutory three-month notice period (or longer where applicable). Sub-statutory notice clauses are void, and termination on shorter notice exposes you to damages.

Key Risk Indicators

  • High Exposure: If all three clauses are absent or non-compliant, the agreement is unlikely to be enforceable as drafted. A comprehensive redraft is recommended.
  • Targeted Gaps: Even a single drafting gap, like missing portfolio measurement methodology, can expose you to material liability at termination.
  • Continuous Evolution: Turkish case law on agency disputes continues to develop. An annual review keeps your contract aligned with the latest TCC jurisprudence.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

What is an Equalization Claim (Denkleştirme İstemi) under Turkish Law?

The equalization claim, often referred to as portfolio compensation or clientele fee, is a statutory right granted to agents under Article 122 of the TCC. If the principal continues to derive substantial benefits from the customers brought in by the agent after the agreement ends, the agent is entitled to equitable compensation. This right cannot be waived prior to the termination of the contract.

Can an agent represent competing companies in Turkey?

By default, an agent holds an exclusivity right and cannot act for competing enterprises in the same territory or branch of trade unless the principal gives explicit consent. Conversely, the principal is also restricted from appointing multiple agents in the same territory for the same trade branch unless mutually agreed in writing.

Are post-contractual non-compete clauses valid in Turkish agency agreements?

Yes, post-contractual non-compete obligations (Rekabet Yasağı) are valid under the TCC. However, they must be strictly limited to a maximum of two years, confined to the agent's specific territory and customer group, and agreed upon in writing. Furthermore, the principal must pay equitable compensation to the agent for the duration of the non-compete restriction.

Does an agent have the authority to sign contracts on behalf of the principal?

Generally, an agent's default authority is limited to mediating and facilitating contracts. To legally bind the principal by signing contracts on their behalf, the agent must be granted explicit, written "contract-making authority" (Sözleşme Yapma Yetkisi) by the principal.
