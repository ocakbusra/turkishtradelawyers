KVKK Prohibits Biometric Data Processing for Employee Attendance Tracking

Data Privacy & Compliance June 2, 2026 6 min read

The Turkish Personal Data Protection Board (KVKK) has issued a binding Principal Decision published in the Official Gazette on June 2, 2026, strictly prohibiting the use of biometric identification systems for employee attendance tracking.

This ruling requires immediate attention from all foreign and domestic employers operating in Türkiye who currently utilize fingerprint scanners, facial recognition, or iris tracking systems to monitor employee working hours.

Important Update: The KVKK has definitively established that biometric data cannot be used for tracking employee entry, exit, and working hours in the Turkish employment landscape. Transitioning to alternative, less intrusive systems is now mandatory.

Legal Rationale and Board Findings

Under the Turkish Personal Data Protection Law No. 6698, biometric data is classified as special category personal data. Processing such data is subject to strict limitations and requires either explicit statutory authorization or the explicit consent of the data subject.

The KVKK’s decision establishes the following critical compliance standards for the Turkish employment landscape:

  • Invalidity of Explicit Consent: The Board ruled that explicit consent obtained in an employer-employee relationship is generally invalid for biometric attendance tracking. Due to the inherent structural power imbalance, an employee's consent cannot be considered freely given, as refusal might result in negative consequences for the employee.
  • Violation of the Proportionality Principle: Even if explicit consent were hypothetically valid, using biometric data solely to track working hours violates the general principle of proportionality outlined in Article 4 of the Law.
  • Administrative Purpose vs. Intrusive Means: The Board noted that attendance tracking is a limited administrative objective. Using highly sensitive, irreversible biometric data for this purpose constitutes an unnecessarily intensive interference with privacy rights.
  • Lack of Explicit Statutory Basis: While Turkish Labor Law requires employers to track and document working hours, it does not explicitly authorize the use of biometric systems for this purpose.

Mandatory Alternatives and Sanctions

The KVKK explicitly directs data controllers to abandon biometric tracking in favor of less intrusive, alternative methods.

Approved alternatives for monitoring employee attendance include:

  • PIN-based or encrypted card systems.
  • RFID/NFC identity cards.
  • Traditional signature and paper-based attendance sheets.
  • Manual entry under supervisor oversight.

Failure to comply with this Principal Decision constitutes a breach of the data controller's obligation to ensure appropriate security levels under Article 12 of the Law. The KVKK has formally announced that non-compliant employers will face administrative sanctions pursuant to Article 18.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

Can we continue using fingerprint or facial recognition scanners if our employees sign detailed, written consent forms?

No. The KVKK has definitively established that explicit consent is insufficient for this specific data processing activity. The Board views the employer-employee relationship as inherently imbalanced, meaning consent is not freely given. Furthermore, the practice inherently violates the proportionality principle regardless of consent.

We use facial recognition for building access and security, not just attendance. Does this decision apply?

This Principal Decision specifically addresses biometric data processing for the purpose of "attendance tracking" (mesai takibi). However, the Board's strict interpretation of the proportionality principle indicates that using biometrics for general office access carries severe compliance risks. Turkish law requires adopting the least intrusive method to achieve a legitimate aim.

Are there any exceptions to this rule?

The current decision does not outline industry-specific exceptions for attendance tracking. The ruling is rooted in the fundamental principles of the Law No. 6698, specifically the requirement that data processing must be limited, relevant, and proportionate to its purpose.

What steps must our local HR and compliance teams take immediately?

Employers must immediately cease using biometric systems for recording employee entry, exit, and working hours. Companies must transition to the alternative tracking methods explicitly endorsed by the KVKK, such as RFID cards or PIN systems.

What are the penalties for non-compliance?

The KVKK stated that failure to implement the necessary technical and administrative measures in line with this decision will result in legal action against the data controllers under Article 18 of the Law No. 6698. This involves substantial administrative fines.
